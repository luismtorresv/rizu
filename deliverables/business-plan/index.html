    Marketing and Sales Strategy

    The marketing strategy for Rizu focuses on introducing the platform to organizations seeking a simplified approach to private cloud management through OpenStack. The target audience consists mainly of small and medium-sized enterprises, educational institutions, and IT departments that need accessible cloud tools without the complexity of traditional interfaces. This audience has been selected because of its increasing interest in adopting cloud-based infrastructure and its demand for solutions that reduce training and operational barriers.

    Promotion will rely on digital channels aligned with professional and technical user habits, including campaigns on LinkedIn and participation in online communities dedicated to cloud technologies. Demonstration workshops and technical webinars will be used to showcase Rizu’s features and build trust through direct interaction. Strategic collaborations with local technology providers will help extend market reach and create referral networks.

    Sales will follow a business-to-business model supported by direct outreach and an online trial system to facilitate product adoption. The financial plan includes projected costs for digital marketing, event participation, and content production, ensuring consistency between the promotional activities and the overall financial structure.

    Risks and Possible Eventualities

    "Anything that can go wrong will go wrong."

    —Murphy’s Law

    The development and deployment of Rizu face several risks derived from the team’s limited scale, the technical nature of the product, and the competitive environment in which it operates.

    Technical and Development Risks

    Given that the team is composed of undergraduate students, there is a risk of limited experience in advanced OpenStack configurations, production-level security, and long-term maintenance. Integration errors or lack of testing resources could lead to unstable releases or delayed delivery. Dependence on open-source frameworks also implies exposure to external changes in APIs or upstream versions that may disrupt functionality.

    Financial and Resource Risks

    As an early-stage student project, Rizu operates with scarce financial backing. Insufficient capital could affect infrastructure costs, marketing efforts, or support capacity. Delays in obtaining grants, sponsorships, or early customers could threaten sustainability before reaching operational breakeven. Likewise, the absence of full-time staff makes the project vulnerable to turnover or graduation-related availability issues.

    Market and Adoption Risks

    OpenStack users represent a specialized and technically demanding audience. Many organizations already rely on Horizon or have internal tools adapted to their infrastructure. Rizu’s value proposition—simplicity and accessibility—may not be sufficient to shift established user habits. There is also a risk of limited visibility in the global market due to low marketing budgets and regional constraints.

    Operational and Legal Risks

    Limited institutional backing may complicate formal contracting, intellectual property registration, and compliance with data protection standards. Operating in Colombia, where access to venture support and cloud infrastructure is still developing, may restrict the ability to scale or form alliances with hosting providers.

    Note

    These risks are not necessarily detrimental if recognized early and managed through conservative planning, technical mentorship, and progressive release cycles.

    Conclusion on the Project's Viability

    “It is better to have a bad plan than no plan.”

    —Garry Kasparov, How Life Imitates Chess

    From a technical standpoint, Rizu is viable. The team’s background in web development and cloud deployment provides a sound foundation for building a functional, minimal dashboard tailored to a defined user segment. The open-source nature of OpenStack lowers entry barriers by allowing integration with existing frameworks without major licensing costs.

    Economically, the business plan projects short-term losses but demonstrates positive growth within three years, reflecting a credible path to profitability under modest operational expenses. The target market—small and medium enterprises with existing hardware—reduces customer-side investment needs and supports the feasibility of adoption.

    Operationally, the plan remains feasible for a small team provided that workflow automation, code maintainability, and customer support processes are prioritized. Success depends on disciplined project management, consistent testing, and gradual scaling of support operations.

    While Rizu entails notable risks inherent to its scale and context, the project is technically achievable, economically sustainable, and operatively manageable within the proposed scope. With proper risk mitigation, it presents a realistic opportunity for implementation and moderate commercial success.
